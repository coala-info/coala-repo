# How does PICRUSt2 work? What does it do?

[Jump to bottom](#wiki-pages-box)

Robyn Wright edited this page Mar 31, 2025
·
[2 revisions](/picrust/picrust2/wiki/How-does-PICRUSt2-work%3F-What-does-it-do%3F/_history)

PICRUSt2 aims to *predict* the functional content of microbial communities based on amplicon/marker gene sequences. Very simply, PICRUSt2 takes information about which microbes are present in a sample and predicts what these microbes might be doing. PICRUSt2 compares the marker gene sequences from a sample to known genomes from similar microbes in order to predict the genes that are likely present and what functions they might perform. The most common functions that we are trying to predict are enzymes ([**E**nzyme **C**ommission [EC] numbers](https://zymvol.com/2024/01/30/whats-an-ec-number-how-to-interpret-it/) or [KEGG orthologs](https://www.genome.jp/kegg/ko.html)).

We also recommend having a read through of the other pages on this Wiki (see the right-hand sidebar), particularly the page on the [Key limitations of PICRUSt2](https://github.com/picrust/picrust2/wiki/Key-Limitations).

## Input

In order to use PICRUSt2 you will need some processed amplicon sequencing data. You will need representative sequences in [fasta format](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/FASTA_format) and a feature table. If you have your raw sequences in [fastq format](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/FASTQ_format) and haven't processed them yet, you are not ready to run PICRUSt2 yet. [QIIME2](https://qiime2.org/) contains many different options for processing your data, has well-documented tutorials, and has extensive support available through their [forum](https://forum.qiime2.org/). There are also other options for processing your data, but we recommend starting there and then coming back to PICRUSt2 when you are ready and have your representative sequences and feature table.

#### 1. Representative sequences

Most commonly these are **A**mplicon **S**equence **V**ariants (ASVs) or **O**perational **T**axonomic **U**nits (OTUs) - in a fasta file. This should look something like this:

## PICRUSt2 workflow

PICRUSt2 is carrying out several steps in order to get functional predictions for your data:

#### 1. Finding the reference genomes that best match your sequences

##### This is done by:

* Creating a multiple sequence alignment of your representative sequences against the PICRUSt2 database reference sequences.
* Inserting the representative sequences into the PICRUSt2 database reference phylogenetic trees for bacteria and archaea.
* Determining whether each representative sequence fits best in the bacterial or the archaeal phylogenetic tree. To do this, Nearest Sequenced Taxon Indices (NSTI) are calculated between your sequences and the PICRUSt2 database reference sequences. PICRUSt2 then looks at whether this value is smaller for the bacterial or the archaeal reference sequences.

#### 2. Predict the gene family copy number for each of your sequnences (also called hidden-state prediction)

##### This will be done for each functional category that you tell PICRUSt2 to use (provided that they exist in the PICRUSt2 database). Options here are:

* [EC numbers](https://zymvol.com/2024/01/30/whats-an-ec-number-how-to-interpret-it/)
* [KEGG orthologs](https://www.genome.jp/kegg/ko.html)
* [BiGG reactions](http://bigg.ucsd.edu/)
* [**C**arbohydrate-**A**ctive En**zy**mes (CAZy)](https://www.cazy.org/)
* Gene names
* [Gene Ontology (GO) annotations](https://geneontology.org/)
* [Protein Family (Pfam)](https://pfam.xfam.org/)

##### This is done by:

* Looking at the gene content of the surrounding tree branches
* Predicting the gene content of each representative sequence based on the ancestral state
* There are several different methods for doing this, and [maximum parsimony](https://www.rdocumentation.org/packages/castor/versions/1.6.1/topics/hsp_max_parsimony) is the default

#### 3. Determine gene family abundance per sample

##### This is done by:

* Normalising the input sequence abundance table (feature table) by the marker gene copy number (e.g. for 16S sequencing data, the sequence abundance table will be normalised by the predicted 16S copy number)
* Multiply the abundances of each gene family by the abundance of the representative sequences to determine the predicted abundance per sample

#### 4. Infer pathway abundances

We are often interested in the abundance of functional pathways within our samples. These are calculated using the same approach as in [HUMAnN](http://huttenhower.sph.harvard.edu/humann2) based on the abundances of gene families (EC numbers by default) that can be linked to reactions within pathways ([MetaCyc pathways](https://metacyc.org/) by default).

##### This is done by:

* Regrouping EC numbers to MetaCyc pathways
* Calculating the harmonic mean of key reactions within the pathway

##### More details:

Pathway abundances are calculated as the harmonic mean of the key reaction abundances in a sample (the distinction of "key" reactions is for structured pathways like MetaCyc, which have both optional and key reactions). There are some other subtleties as well, such as gap filling by default (setting the reaction/gene with the lowest abundance to be that of the second-lowest abundance), and including optional reactions to some degree as well. Specifically, optional reactions with abundance > harmonic mean based on key reactions alone are included in the calculation. Last, there can be nested pathways/sub-pathways in the structures, which are computed recursively (i.e., the abundance of a sub-pathway is computed and then its abundance is treated just like that of any other reaction). This overall approach was used to keep the output as similar as possible to HUMAnN2.

The MetaCyc reaction abundances are based on the predicted EC number abundances (the mapping file `ec_level4_to_metacyc_rxn.tsv`, which is in `picrust2/default_files/pathway_mapfiles`, shows how they are linked). Note that mainly the EC numbers and MetaCyc reactions are 1:1 links, but not all.

Because the MetaCyc reactions are based on regrouping the predicted EC numbers the resulting pathway predictions also partially correspond to the origin

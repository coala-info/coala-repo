## Abstract

Translation is principally regulated at the initiation stage. The development of the translation initiation (TI) sequencing (TI-seq) technique has enabled the global mapping of TIs and revealed unanticipated complex translational landscapes in metazoans. Despite the wide adoption of TI-seq, there is no computational tool currently available for analyzing TI-seq data. To fill this gap, we develop a comprehensive toolkit named Ribo-TISH, which allows for detecting and quantitatively comparing TIs across conditions from TI-seq data. Ribo-TISH can also predict novel open reading frames (ORFs) from regular ribosome profiling (rRibo-seq) data and outperform several established methods in both computational efficiency and prediction accuracy. Applied to published TI-seq/rRibo-seq data sets, Ribo-TISH uncovers a novel signature of elevated mitochondrial translation during amino-acid deprivation and predicts novel ORFs in 5′UTRs, long noncoding RNAs, and introns. These successful applications demonstrate the power of Ribo-TISH in extracting biological insights from TI-seq/rRibo-seq data.

## Introduction

Translation is an essential step of gene expression. It is tightly controlled[1](/articles/s41467-017-01981-8#ref-CR1 "Sonenberg, N. & Hinnebusch, A. G. Regulation of translation initiation in eukaryotes: mechanisms and biological targets. Cell
                           136, 731–745 (2009).") and is crucial to numerous developmental[2](/articles/s41467-017-01981-8#ref-CR2 "Curtis, D., Lehmann, R. & Zamore, P. D. Translational regulation in development. Cell
                           81, 171–178 (1995).") and physiological processes[3](/articles/s41467-017-01981-8#ref-CR3 "Buffington, S. A., Huang, W. & Costa-Mattioli, M. Translational control in synaptic plasticity and cognitive dysfunction. Annu. Rev. Neurosci.
                           37, 17–38 (2014)."), [4](/articles/s41467-017-01981-8#ref-CR4 "Spriggs, K. A., Bushell, M. & Willis, A. E. Translational regulation of gene expression during conditions of cell stress. Mol. Cell
                           40, 228–237 (2010)."), such as early embryogenesis[2](/articles/s41467-017-01981-8#ref-CR2 "Curtis, D., Lehmann, R. & Zamore, P. D. Translational regulation in development. Cell
                           81, 171–178 (1995).") and stress responses[4](/articles/s41467-017-01981-8#ref-CR4 "Spriggs, K. A., Bushell, M. & Willis, A. E. Translational regulation of gene expression during conditions of cell stress. Mol. Cell
                           40, 228–237 (2010)."), [5](/articles/s41467-017-01981-8#ref-CR5 "Starck, S. R. et al. Translation from the 5′ untranslated region shapes the integrated stress response. Science
                           351, aad3867 (2016)."), where translational control of the pre-existing mRNAs can change the final protein abundance more rapidly than the synthesis of new mRNAs. The dysregulation of translation is associated with many diseases, such as anemia[6](/articles/s41467-017-01981-8#ref-CR6 "Flygare, J. & Karlsson, S. Diamond-Blackfan anemia: erythropoiesis lost in translation. Blood
                           109, 3152–3154 (2007)."), neurological disorders[7](/articles/s41467-017-01981-8#ref-CR7 "Scheper, G. C., van der Knaap, M. S. & Proud, C. G. Translation matters: protein synthesis defects in inherited disease. Nat. Rev. Genet.
                           8, 711–723 (2007)."), and cancer[8](/articles/s41467-017-01981-8#ref-CR8 "Silvera, D., Formenti, S. C. & Schneider, R. J. Translational control in cancer. Nat. Rev. Cancer
                           10, 254–266 (2010).").

The development of the ribosome profiling (ribo-seq) technique has enabled the high-resolution measurement of translation on a genome-wide scale[1](#ref-CR1 "Sonenberg, N. & Hinnebusch, A. G. Regulation of translation initiation in eukaryotes: mechanisms and biological targets. Cell
                           136, 731–745 (2009)."),[2](#ref-CR2 "Curtis, D., Lehmann, R. & Zamore, P. D. Translational regulation in development. Cell
                           81, 171–178 (1995)."),[3](/articles/s41467-017-01981-8#ref-CR3 "Buffington, S. A., Huang, W. & Costa-Mattioli, M. Translational control in synaptic plasticity and cognitive dysfunction. Annu. Rev. Neurosci.
                           37, 17–38 (2014)."). The basic procedure of ribo-seq is to perform deep sequencing of the DNA libraries converted from the ribosome-protected mRNA fragments (RPFs, also termed ribosome footprints) that are generated by RNase digestion, to determine the occupancy of translating ribosomes on a given mRNA. There are several variations of the ribo-seq technique that use different translation inhibitors[4](#ref-CR4 "Spriggs, K. A., Bushell, M. & Willis, A. E. Translational regulation of gene expression during conditions of cell stress. Mol. Cell
                           40, 228–237 (2010)."),[5](#ref-CR5 "Starck, S. R. et al. Translation from the 5′ untranslated region shapes the integrated stress response. Science
                           351, aad3867 (2016)."),[6](/articles/s41467-017-01981-8#ref-CR6 "Flygare, J. & Karlsson, S. Diamond-Blackfan anemia: erythropoiesis lost in translation. Blood
                           109, 3152–3154 (2007)."). Regular ribo-seq (rRibo-seq) utilizes cycloheximide (CHX)[4](/articles/s41467-017-01981-8#ref-CR4 "Spriggs, K. A., Bushell, M. & Willis, A. E. Translational regulation of gene expression during conditions of cell stress. Mol. Cell
                           40, 228–237 (2010)."), a translation elongation inhibitor, to freeze all translating ribosomes. Recent studies using CHX-based rRibo-seq revealed pervasive translation in the genomic regions that are beyond the annotated protein-coding regions[9](#ref-CR9 "Ingolia, N. T. et al. Ribosome profiling reveals pervasive translation outside of annotated protein-coding genes. Cell Rep.
                           8, 1365–1379 (2014)."),[10](#ref-CR10 "Bazzini, A. A. et al. Identification of small ORFs in vertebrates using ribosome footprinting and evolutionary conservation. EMBO J.
                           33, 981–993 (2014)."),[11](#ref-CR11 "Aspden, J. L. et al Extensive translation of small open reading frames revealed by poly-ribo-seq. eLife
                           3, e03528 (2014)."),[12](#ref-CR12 "Ji, Z., Song, R., Regev, A. & Struhl, K. Many lncRNAs, 5′UTRs, and pseudogenes are translated and some are likely to express functional proteins. eLife 4, e08890 (2015)."),[13](/articles/s41467-017-01981-8#ref-CR13 "Chew, G. L. et al. Ribosome profiling reveals resemblance between long non-coding RNAs and 5′ leaders of coding RNAs. Development
                           140, 2828–2834 (2013)."). These newly discovered translated regions not only include small open reading frames (smORFs, ≤100 amino acids) in intragenic regions of protein-coding genes (PCGs), such as those in the 5′ untranslated region (5′UTR; upstream ORFs, uORFs) or 3′UTR (downstream ORFs, dORFs) but also include the smORFs within long noncoding RNAs (lncRNAs)[14](/articles/s41467-017-01981-8#ref-CR14 "Rinn, J. L. & Chang, H. Y. Genome regulation by long noncoding RNAs. Annu. Rev. Biochem.
                           81, 145–166 (2012)."), [15](/articles/s41467-017-01981-8#ref-CR15 "Slavoff, S. A. et al. Peptidomic discovery of short open reading frame-encoded peptides in human cells. Nat. Chem. Biol.
                           9, 59–64 (2013)."), which were not expected to encode any sizable proteins. The human genome encodes over 15,000 lncRNA genes. Based on rRibo-seq data, it has been estimated that ~40% of lncRNA genes may contain translated smORFs[12](/articles/s41467-017-01981-8#ref-CR12 "Ji, Z., Song, R., Regev, A. & Struhl, K. Many lncRNAs, 5′UTRs, and pseudogenes are translated and some are likely to express functional proteins. eLife 4, e08890 (2015)."). A few of the smORFs within lncRNAs have been shown to play essential developmental or physiological roles in evolutionarily distant species[16](#ref-CR16 "Magny, E. G. et al. Conserved regulation of cardiac calcium uptake by peptides encoded in small open reading frames. Science
                           341, 1116–1120 (2013)."),[17](#ref-CR17 "Nelson, B. R. et al. A peptide encoded by a transcript annotated as long noncoding RNA enhances SERCA activity in muscle. Science
                           351, 271–275 (2016)."),[18](#ref-CR18 "Pauli, A. et al. Toddler: an embryonic signal that promotes cell movement via Apelin receptors. Science
                           343, 1248636 (2014)."),[19](/articles/s41467-017-01981-8#ref-CR19 "Anderson, D. M. et al. A micropeptide encoded by a putative long noncoding RNA regulates muscle performance. Cell. 160, 595–606 (2015).").

Translation is largely regulated at the initiation stage[20](/articles/s41467-017-01981-8#ref-CR20 "Jackson, R. J., Hellen, C. U. & Pestova, T. V. The mechanism of eukaryotic translation initiation and principles of its regulation. Nat. Rev. Mol. Cell Biol.
                           11, 113–127 (2010)."). Therefore, elucidating the mechanism and regulation of translation initiation (TI) is fundamental to our understanding of translational regulation. The use of the translation inhibitor lactimidomycin (LTM)[21](/articles/s41467-017-01981-8#ref-CR21 "Lee, S. et al. Global mapping of translation initiation sites in mammalian cells at single-nucleotide resolution. Proc. Natl Acad. Sci. USA
                           109, E2424–E2432 (2012).") or harringtonine (Harr)[22](/articles/s41467-017-01981-8#ref-CR22 "Ingolia, N. T., Lareau, L. F. & Weissman, J. S. Ribosome profiling of mouse embryonic stem cells reveals the complexity and dynamics of

# Tips and Tricks

This page serves as a location to provide some details on some of the nitty gritty aspects of bronko's algorithm and how to get the most out of it for your own data. We try to provide practical advice on two primary challenges: 1) building a bronko database and 2) identifying low-quality or divergent samples

Note

If you have any other questions specific to your data or use case, please raise an issue or email me at rdd4@rice.edu!

## Building a bronko database

One of the most important steps to ensure that bronko performs well is to select a reference set for profiling. The primary goal when considering what sequences to include in your sample is that they should span the genomic diversity of your samples.

For instance, if you already know the lineage(s) / nextstrain clade(s) of your viruses in your samples (ex. Sars-CoV-2 25C (XFG)), then you can download some of these viruses from [NCBI Virus](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/labs/virus/vssi/#/) or [Nextstrain](https://nextstrain.org/) or [Gisaid](https://gisaid.org/). You can then use `bronko build` to build a database of these viruses and then use `bronko call` to call variants in your sequencing data against the database you built. If you also already have assemblies from some of your samples, you could incorporate them into the database if they are complete. This is often the best way to ensure you get good results on your given sample set.

Tip

bronko will not throw an error if you have poor-quality viral assemblies. We recommend filtering for complete genomes with no ambiguous bases (this should be fine for most common viruses). Poor-quality or partial viral assemblies could have unintended downstream affects

In cases where you do not know the specific strain(s) of your samples, but rather the general species (or just want to analyze a diverse set of samples), you can build a database with a larger genome set. One way that we have found to do this is simply to select one-two high-quality reference sequences from every major lineage of a virus. In our paper, we used the heuristic of the first and most recently sampled complete reference sequence for each lineage. Note that bronko does not inherently prevent you from profiling multiple species using a single database, but this will increase the size of your database significantly.

Other important considerations:

* Memory usage naturally increases as you include more references. 100 Sars-CoV-2 references took about 9GB for me.
* Larger databases take longer to load into memory, but then variant calling is not significantly affected if you have sufficient threads
* Most times you will not need higher resolution than the lineage

## High level quality control

In this section, we will describe some common things you should look for to 1) identify poor quality samples 2) identify lack of a good representative sequence in the database.

After running `bronko call` on a set of samples, the first file you should look at is the tsv overview (by default `bronko_overview.tsv`). This file contains a lot of important information (described in detail [here](https://github.com/treangenlab/bronko/wiki/Description-of-Outputs)). Before looking into any of the vcf files, you should look for a few things:

1. Are there any samples with low sequencing depth or breadth? Typically for high quality amplicon sequencing samples you should expect near 98-100% breadth and depth > 100-500x, but this may vary depending on the protocol and organism.
2. Are there any samples with tons of major variants with respect to the genome size? This can be defined in a few ways, but generally once identity falls below something more than 99.5-99.8%, you may consider trying to re-evaluate or add more references. For instance even major lineages often do not differ by many total variants (ex. Omicron differs by ~50 variants from Alpha variants of Sars-CoV-2, representing ~99.8 identity). The main consideration is that more major variants can disrupt the minor variant calling process.

Tip

One way to get a better reference, particularly if you have a number of samples with high divergence, is to simply assemble one of them, and then add the consensus genome to your reference set.

Please note that viruses are diverse and each one is different. We have not tested bronko on every virus that has ever been observed, so there may be additional challenges with less characterized human viruses.

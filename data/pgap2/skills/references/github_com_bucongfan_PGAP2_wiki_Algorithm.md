# Algorithm

The core idea of PGAP2 is to perform a preliminary clustering of orthologous genes based on gene similarity and synteny networks. Subsequently, these clusters are refined using internal features, addressing the limitations of hard thresholds for similarity and synteny in handling highly variable clusters. The overall workflow can be summarized as follows:

PGAP2 maintains two networks and identifies orthologous genes based on the relationships between these networks:

* **Gene Identity Network**: Describes the sequence similarity between genes. Higher similarity increases the likelihood that the genes are orthologs.
* **Gene Synteny Network**: Describes the adjacent distance between genes. More conserved synteny indicates a higher likelihood of orthology.

# Gene Identitiy Network

In the **gene identity network**, nodes represent clustered representative sequences, and edges denote the sequence similarity between these representative sequences.

To construct this network, clustering tools like **CD-HIT** or **MMseqs2** are used to perform two levels of clustering on the entire set of protein sequences: one for duplication and another for orthology. This approach is consistent with the logic of most algorithms and serves two main purposes:

1. **Reducing computational cost**: By clustering similar genes, the time spent on sequence alignments is significantly decreased.
2. **Identifying recent duplication events**: PGAP2 introduces a similarity threshold, **duplication**, which is even higher than the orthology threshold. Clusters exceeding this threshold are considered potential **recent duplication events**.

Next, alignment tools like **BLAST** or **DIAMOND** are used to perform all-against-all alignments of the representative gene sequences. The resulting data is then processed with the **Markov Cluster Algorithm (MCL)** for initial filtering, retaining reliable clusters to form the similarity network.

# Gene Synteny Network

In the **gene synteny network**, nodes represent clustered representative sequences, and edges capture the positional relationships between these representative sequences and their members.

Using the coordinate positions from the structural annotations of the strains, all adjacent genes are connected. In this network:

* Nodes are the representative sequences.
* Edges indicate the adjacency relationship among members within nodes.

This means that any connected nodes must correspond to adjacent genes. The closer two nodes are, the smaller their actual distance in the genome. This step aligns with the logic of graph-based algorithms such as **Panaroo** and **PPanGGOLiN**.

# Resolving "Sticky" Nodes

PGAP2 further processes **"sticky" nodes** that are similar but not adjacent. These nodes may have high sequence similarity internally but are not connected by positional adjacency. If left unprocessed, these nodes could act as "black holes," linking two distant nodes with low actual synteny.

To address this, PGAP2 recursively examines all such sticky nodes. Based on the **Conserved Gene Neighbor (CGN)** rule, these nodes are split to ensure that:

1. All nodes are free of internal adjacency conflicts.
2. The synteny network remains acyclic within each strain.

After processing, the nodes in the **gene identity network** are updated to remain consistent with the refined **gene synteny network**.

# Network Association

By interacting with both networks, PGAP2 identifies **clusters** that simultaneously meet the requirements for sequence similarity and synteny conservation, designating these as candidate clusters. The process involves the following steps:

1. **Traverse Clusters in the Similarity Network**
   PGAP2 iterates through each cluster in the **gene identity network**, where each cluster consists of nodes forming at least one connected subgraph based on sequence similarity.
2. **Search for Connected Subgraphs in the Synteny Network**
   Within each similarity-based cluster, PGAP2 examines the **gene synteny network** to identify subgraphs where nodes are connected based on synteny.
3. **Return Candidate Clusters**
   PGAP2 outputs subgraphs that are connected in both the similarity and synteny networks. Each node in these candidate clusters must have at least one edge satisfying both the **sequence similarity** and **synteny conservation** thresholds.

These candidate clusters represent robust groups that meet the dual criteria of gene similarity and positional conservation, ensuring they are biologically meaningful for downstream analysis.

# Refining Candidate Cluster

Candidate clusters meet the minimum requirements for **sequence similarity** and **synteny conservation**, making them sufficient for identifying orthologous genes in highly conserved clusters, such as core genes. However, for clusters with higher internal variability, like shell genes, hard thresholds for homology and synteny alone are not precise enough for classification. PGAP2 refines these clusters further by analyzing their internal features.

PGAP2 considers three key features to refine and classify clusters:

## 1. **Cluster Diversity**

* **Purpose**: To limit the variability within a cluster, ensuring its internal similarity differences are not excessively large.
* **Implementation**: PGAP2 collects the maximum similarity difference (range) between genes in high-confidence clusters.
  + **High-confidence clusters** are defined as those without duplicate strains after initial clustering (e.g., core genes).
  + The smallest of these ranges is set as the minimum **cluster diversity threshold**.

---

## 2. **Cluster Connectivity**

* **Purpose**: To ensure that clusters have an appropriate degree of connectivity for accurate classification.
* **Levels**:
  + **Strict Connectivity**: All nodes within a cluster must form a fully connected graph. This is ideal for identifying orthologous genes in core genomes.
  + **Soft Connectivity**: Only one edge connecting nodes is required. This is suitable for partitioning mobile elements like plasmids.
* **Flexibility**: The level of connectivity can be adjusted based on the classification needs.

---

## 3. **Bidirectional Best Hit (BBH)**

* **Purpose**: For clusters containing duplicated genes, ensure that the duplication events occurred after the divergence of orthologous genes.
* **Principle**: Any two genes within the cluster must satisfy the **BBH criterion**, confirming that their duplication reflects recent events.

# Iterative Refinement Process

PGAP2 iteratively refines clusters using these features:

1. It starts with the node pairs with the highest homology.
2. Nodes are evaluated pair by pair for the three features (diversity, connectivity, and BBH).
3. If a pair satisfies these criteria, their corresponding nodes are merged.
4. The process continues until no remaining pairs meet the criteria.

# Handling the Impact of Insertion Sequences

To minimize the influence of **insertion sequences** on the classification of orthologous genes, PGAP2 performs an additional merging step. In this step, all sequences with a **similarity greater than 0.99** are merged, regardless of their synteny (neighbor conservation).

This approach is justified because insertion sequences tend to be highly variable in terms of their insertion locations within the genome, resulting in a lack of neighbor conservation. By focusing solely on sequence similarity, PGAP2 ensures that these high-similarity sequences, often caused by recent duplication or horizontal transfer, are correctly grouped, preventing them from artificially splitting orthologous gene clusters.

More specifically, given community composition data, Phylogenize2 links genes in microbial genomes to either microbial prevalence in, or specificity for, a given environment, while also taking into account an important potential confounder: the phylogenetic relationships between microbes. Phylogenize2 comes with web , QIIME 2, and R interfaces. The method is described fully in Bradley, Nayfach, and Pollard (2018) . Why Use phylogenize 1. Linking Gene Content to Host Traits Phylogenize was designed to test whether the presence/absence of genes (or gene families) across microbial genomes correlates with host-associated phenotypes or environments. Example: Does a particular microbial gene cluster track with mammalian gut vs. insect gut habitats? This enables hypothesis generation about functional adaptations in microbes tied to specific niches. 2. Phylogeny-Aware Association Testing Microbial traits are not independent—closely related species share genes simply due to shared ancestry. Phylogenize uses phylogenetic regression models to correct for shared evolutionary history. This avoids spurious associations that would arise if you ignored relatedness and treated each genome independently. 3. Integration of Large Genome Catalogs The pipeline can operate directly on big pangenome resources ( GLOBDB and MGnify genomes ), allowing: Broad-scale screens across thousands of genomes. The ability to test associations at the level of individual genes, KO pathways, or GO terms . Extension to custom genome datasets (e.g., isolates from your own study). 4. Host–Microbiome Evolution Questions Phylogenize is useful for researchers interested in: Holobiont evolution (how microbes adapt alongside their hosts). Gene gain/loss analyses in specific environments. Ecological differentiation (e.g., soil vs. host-associated bacteria). 5. User-Facing Tools Both an R package ( phylogenize ) and a Snakemake/HPC workflow ( phylogenize-db-prep + extensions) are available. Supports exploratory analyses and reproducible pipelines on high-performance clusters. Results can be summarized in RMarkdown reports , with visualizations of enriched traits or pathways.
